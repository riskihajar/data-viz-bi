ABSTRACT Educational Data Mining and learning analytics have gained significant prominence in recent
years, garnering attention from researchers worldwide. This is primarily due to their potential to improve
decision-making processes within higher education. This study utilizes Educational Data Mining to suggest
a clustering-based approach for classifying students based on their academic performance, specifically
focusing on their interactions within Learning Management Systems in blended learning settings. Log data
from the Learning Management System Moodle platform was analyzed to determine if valuable insights
could be derived from student activities without the need for personal or invasive data. Given the large
dataset, Principal Component Analysis and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection were employed
for dimensionality reduction. Three unsupervised learning models—BIRCH, DBSCAN, and GMM—were
then applied to identify distinct student clusters. The results from the comparison revealed that the BIRCH
algorithm was the most effective in accurately categorizing students based on their activities. Further analysis
of identified clusters revealed a strong correlation between student engagement and academic outcomes,
with two high-risk clusters demonstrating the highest dropout rates. To better understand the reasons behind
the found clusters, the course duration was subsequently divided into intervals, and standard machine
learning methods were applied to predict at-risk students over time, confirming that early identification
of at-risk students using clustering is feasible. This study demonstrates the efficacy of non-invasive data
collection and unsupervised learning models in predicting student success, emphasizing the importance of
temporal and content-specific analysis. The proposed approach leverages clustering algorithms to enhance
the understanding of student behavior by identifying patterns and grouping students with similar learning
and engagement profiles. This supports the development of targeted interventions that are tailored to specific
student needs, ultimately improving retention and success.
INDEX TERMS Educational data mining, learning analytics, clustering analysis, dropout prediction.

1) DATA TRANSFORMATION

Most machine learning algorithms generally require numeric
input and output variables. This restriction must be addressed
when implementing and developing machine learning models. This implies that all characteristics, including categories
or nominal variables, must be transformed into numeric
variables before entering data into the clustering and classification model [23].
Therefore, in the process of data transformation, the
variable Component was mapped to the numerical values:
Assignment: 0, Attendance: 1, Course: 2, Project: 3, Quiz:
4, Study_material: 5. Similarly, Action and Target were also
converted into numerical representations using predefined
mappings, thus enabling these categorical attributes to be
processed by various machine learning algorithms.
Machine learning models are designed to identify relationships between input variables (features) and output variables
(predictions or target outcomes). This means the model uses
input data, such as student engagement metrics, to predict specific outcomes, such as academic performance or
likelihood of course completion. However, the scale and distribution of the domain data may differ for each variable.
Moreover, input variables may have distinct units, which
means they may have different scales. Differences in scaling
among input variables may increase the complexity of the
modeled problem. Large input values can result in a model
that learns large, weighted values. A model with weighted
values is frequently unstable, so it may perform poorly during
learning and be sensitive to input values, resulting in a larger
error. On top of that, normalization was performed using
the StandardScaler or z-transformation and MinMaxScaler
methods to form datasets for three different models. The first
method, StandardScaler, allowed each attribute’s values to
be in the same range so that a comparison could be made.
Z-transformation normalization refers to normalizing every
value in a dataset such that the mean of all values is 0 and the
standard deviation is 1. In the second method, MinMaxScaler
converted all attributes into a range [0, 1], which means the
minimum value of the attribute was zero, and the maximum
value of the attribute was one [24].

4) DiMENSIONALITY REDUCTION

In the data preparation phase of the analysis, dimensionality reduction was crucial for managing the computational
complexity and enhancing the visualization of the examined
educational dataset. Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
was initially applied to reduce the dimensionality of the
examined dataset while retaining 95% of the variance. This
variance retention threshold dictated the number of compoVOLUME 12, 2024

nents selected by PCA, effectively condensing the dataset into
a lower-dimensional space that preserves the most informative features for clustering.
Figure 5 presents two graphs to illustrate the process of
determining the number of principal components. Figure 5a,
‘‘% Explained Variance,’’ displays the individual contribution
of each principal component to the dataset variance. It is
evident that the four components account for the highest
variance, with each subsequent component contributing progressively less.
On the other hand, Figure 5b, ‘‘% Cumulative Variance,’’
has a marked point labeled ‘‘CV = 0.954’’ at the fifth
component, which implies that approximately 95.4% of the
total variance has been captured by the first five principal
components. This suggests that selecting six components
would retain most of the information contained in the original
dataset while substantially reducing its dimensionality.
Upon identifying the six optimal components through
PCA, which accounted for 95.4% of the variance, Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) was
applied to visualize the data in a two-dimensional space.
UMAP is renowned for its effectiveness in preserving both
local and global data structures and provides an insightful
representation for further analysis. The utilization of UMAP
after PCA serves two main purposes. First, it simplifies
the complexity of multi-dimensional data, making it more
manageable for computational processes. Second, and most
importantly for this analysis, it facilitates a more intuitive
visualization of the inherent clustering of the dataset.
The UMAP model was configured with two components to
reduce the high-dimensional data into a two-dimensional representation, making it easier to visually explore and interpret
the complex relationships within the dataset. This dimensionality reduction helps uncover patterns and groupings that are
not easily observable in the original multi-dimensional space,
allowing for a clearer understanding of the underlying structure in the data. The parameter n_neighbors was set to 150,
influencing UMAP’s balance between local and global structure in the data. In contrast, min_dist, set to 0.5, controlled
how tightly UMAP packs the points in the low-dimensional
space. These specific settings were chosen to strike a balance
between the granularity of the clusters and the broad arrangement of the data, allowing for clear differentiation between
clusters while maintaining the overall data distribution. The
resulting two-dimensional embedding crafted by UMAP captures the intrinsic geometry of the high-dimensional space

In recent years, researchers have garnered significant attention to the use of clustering techniques for predicting student
performance. Clustering involves grouping similar objects
into one cluster while distinctly different objects are grouped
into separate clusters. This technique proves particularly
useful when labeled information within a student dataset
is unknown. Moreover, dividing large datasets into smaller,
logical clusters facilitates more straightforward examination
and interpretation by researchers.
Among the various clustering methods, the k-means
algorithm is often a preferred choice due to its simplicity in
implementation and straightforward results. K-means groups
the objects based on their proximity to the centroid of the
clusters. The elbow method is commonly used to determine
the optimal number of clusters. This technique involves calculating the total within-cluster variance—or inertia—for
different numbers of clusters (k) and identifying the point
159344

where the variance curve initially levels off, indicating the
most appropriate value for k.
However, the k-means algorithm, despite its popularity, has
certain limitations, particularly for the type of educational
data analyzed in this study. K-means assumes that clusters
are spherical and equally sized, which may not always reflect
the true structure of complex educational data. Additionally,
it is sensitive to outliers and noise, which can skew the results
when dealing with datasets like student interaction logs. More
advanced clustering techniques, better suited for handling the
nuances of the examined data, were explored due to these
limitations.

BIRCH can automatically adjust to the available computational resources, ensuring that it delivers optimal
clustering quality based on the dataset size. This is a
critical advantage in educational settings where data is
often multi-dimensional and complex.
• DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise): One of DBSCAN’s main advantages is its ability to discover clusters of arbitrary shape
and handle noise effectively. This is especially useful

of interaction. DBSCAN’s sensitivity to density allows
it to differentiate between dense areas of student activity

• GMM (Gaussian Mixture Models): Unlike algorithms

the elbow method, and the silhouette score analysis, were
employed to ascertain the most appropriate clustering solution for the examined educational dataset. The Elbow method
is a heuristic method for determining the number of clusters
in a dataset. It involves plotting the explained variance as a
function of the number of clusters and picking the elbow of
the curve as the number of clusters to use. This method looks
for the point at which the rate of decrease sharply changes,
indicating that additional clusters do not significantly contribute to the explanation of variance.
The Elbow Method (Figure 7) illustrates that as the number of clusters increases, the within-cluster sum of squares
(WCSS) decreases because the data points are closer to the
centroids of their clusters. The point where the decline in
WCSS starts to level off is aimed at indicating that adding
more clusters does not significantly improve the variance
explained by the model in the data.

After employing the BIRCH algorithm to identify four
distinct class labels, the clustered dataset has been utilized

performance. This development focuses on selecting the optimal model to classify students based on accurately generated
performance class labels.
To ensure the robustness and generalizability of the predictive models, 10-fold cross-validation was employed. This

method systematically splits the dataset into 10 folds, with the
model being trained on 9 folds and tested on the remaining

part of the data contributes to both the training and testing phases. The performance of these classification models
is systematically documented in Table 6. To enhance the

outcomes. By analyzing performance at these successive

V. CONCLUSION

The research’s findings emphasize the noticeable impact of
student engagement on academic performance. The BIRCH
algorithm’s ability to classify students into clusters based
on their LMS Moodle activity levels underscores the predictive power of engagement metrics. A nuanced understanding
emerges from the distinct clusters:

J. Pecuchova, M. Drlik: Enhancing the Early Student Dropout Prediction Model

Cluster 0 represents consistent and thorough student
engagement associated with higher grades.
• Cluster 1 indicates moderate engagement and average
performance.
• Cluster 2 is characterized by activity centered around
mid-term assessments and is linked to borderline passing
grades.
• Cluster 3 shows minimal engagement and is associated
with failing grades and a high dropout rate.
The discussion of findings revealed by clustering algorithms elucidates the diverse student engagement strategies
within a blended learning course. Notably, BIRCH’s effective segmentation of student behaviors correlates closely
with academic achievements, from consistent engagement
to minimal interaction patterns. While BIRCH exemplifies
algorithmic precision in capturing student engagement, the
nuanced performance of DBSCAN and GMM highlights the
complexity of student behavior analytics.
Limitations of this study include its concentration on a
single academic institution and course, which may restrict
the generalizability of the findings. When analyzing multiple
courses with different structures across various disciplines,
it becomes challenging to identify consistent or useful
patterns of student behavior, as each course may exhibit
distinct dynamics. Therefore, as taken in this study, a more
focused approach allows deeper insights into individual
course dynamics and offers to refine course structures to
better capture student behavior. However, one approach that
could enhance the predictive model without incorporating
personal data would be considering a student’s performance
in other courses as a contextual factor. This information
could be leveraged to refine predictions in the course under
study, providing a more comprehensive view of a student’s
academic trajectory based solely on their online traces.
In addition, using log files assumes that the records accurately
and comprehensively capture a student’s activity.
Despite these limitations, the study provides a framework
that uses LMS data to predict academic achievement. This
framework gives educators a proactive tool to identify students who may be in danger of underperforming or dropping
out.
Furthermore, as educational datasets continue to grow, particularly with the increasing shift towards online and hybrid
learning environments, the scalability of the chosen clustering algorithms becomes a critical consideration. BIRCH,
by design, scales well with large datasets, making it particularly suited for growing data environments. On the other
hand, DBSCAN’s computational complexity may hinder its
performance when faced with extremely large datasets, as calculating distances between data points can become intensive.
GMM, with its probabilistic nature, may also encounter
computational constraints as dataset size increases. Future
research should explore how these algorithms perform in
larger-scale educational settings, and the development of
more scalable clustering methods could enhance their practical application for institutional-level data analytics.

The generalizability of the findings is another important
factor. Since the study was conducted within the context
of a single institution and course, future studies should
consider replicating this methodology across different institutions and learning environments to better understand how
student engagement patterns differ. However, the use of a
standard LMS Moodle log file in this experiment not only
offers a solid baseline for future studies but also ensures
reproducibility thanks to its widely accepted format. This will
help establish whether the insights from clustering algorithms
like BIRCH, DBSCAN, and GMM hold true across various
educational contexts, improving the applicability of the findings to diverse educational settings.
Future research could benefit from a broader dataset that
includes data collected over multiple teaching periods of the
same course, resulting in larger cohorts. This would help
contextualize their engagement in the specific course being
studied and offer a more robust predictive model without
compromising the focus on online traces. Longitudinal studies would also be beneficial in tracking changes in student
engagement over multiple teaching periods, providing further
insights into the stability and adaptability of the predictive
model. Additionally, as the nature of learning environments
evolves, particularly with the rise of hybrid and fully online
formats, it will be valuable to assess whether the same
engagement patterns identified in this study apply to those
contexts. This would ensure that predictive models are adaptable to various learning formats and can be used to address
emerging challenges in student engagement and retention.

offering a predictive framework that identifies at-risk students using clustering and classification and facilitates timely

the importance of a systematic approach to monitoring student engagement through learning management systems. The

higher academic achievement and sporadic interaction with
lower outcomes calls for educational strategies that promote
regular LMS usage. The drawn conclusion emphasizes the
potential of machine learning in shaping educational policies
and supporting students’ academic journeys, reflecting on
the critical role of data-driven interventions in enhancing the
quality of higher education.

the link between student LMS engagement and academic
